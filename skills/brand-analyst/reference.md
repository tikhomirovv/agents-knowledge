# Brand analysis framework (reference)

Use this document when executing the **brand-analyst** skill. Work through the layers in order unless the user scopes the task to specific layers.

---

## 1. Surface description

Describe objectively:

- What is the product?
- What does it do?
- Who is it for?
- What category does it belong to?
- What are its core features?

Keep this section concise.

---

## 2. Positioning analysis

### What mental category does the product want to occupy?

Examples: "Second brain", "Creative workspace", "AI companion", "Professional developer tool", "Calm productivity system", "Luxury technology", "Minimalist focus environment".

### What does the company want users to feel?

Examples: intelligent, creative, powerful, calm, modern, elite, focused, safe, futuristic, expressive, productive, independent.

### What identity is being sold?

Ask: *What kind of person uses this product?*

Examples: ambitious founder, organized thinker, elite designer, technical power-user, mindful intellectual, creative rebel, rational minimalist.

### What aspiration is being sold?

Examples: becoming smarter, more productive, creative, disciplined, modern, technologically advanced, emotionally aware.

---

## 3. Framing analysis

### What frame transforms the product from a tool into a meaningful concept?

Examples:

- Not "notes app" → "second brain"
- Not "chatbot" → "AI assistant"
- Not "diary" → "mental clarity system"
- Not "task manager" → "calm productivity"

### What words are intentionally avoided?

Analyze: avoided terminology, avoided emotional associations, avoided stereotypes.

Examples: "diary" avoided because childish/cringe; "budgeting" replaced with "financial wellness"; "propaganda" replaced with "public relations".

### What emotional baggage is the brand trying to escape?

Identify: negative cultural associations, outdated stereotypes, psychological resistance, identity conflicts.

---

## 4. Emotional and psychological analysis

### What emotional pain does this product solve?

Examples: chaos, anxiety, lack of control, loneliness, overwhelm, lack of identity, fear of failure, confusion, lack of clarity, creative insecurity.

### What emotional reward does the product provide?

Examples: relief, confidence, clarity, momentum, focus, validation, status, calmness, feeling intelligent, feeling capable.

### What fear is indirectly activated?

Examples: falling behind, being disorganized, being average, missing opportunities, losing control, being irrelevant.

### What fantasy does the product sell?

Examples: "I can become my best self", "I can organize my life", "I can think clearly", "I can work like elite people", "I can become superhuman with AI".

---

## 5. Narrative analysis

### What narrative does the company tell?

Examples: "The future of work", "Humans + AI", "Creative empowerment", "Technology should feel magical", "Productivity without burnout".

### What worldview does the brand promote?

Ask: What does this company believe about people? About work? About technology? About creativity? About intelligence? About society?

### What enemy exists in the narrative?

Every strong brand has an implicit enemy.

Examples: chaos, complexity, old software, corporate rigidity, distraction, inefficiency, outdated thinking.

---

## 6. UX and interface psychology

### How does the UI make the user feel?

Examples: calm, powerful, technical, playful, luxurious, minimal, experimental, intelligent.

### What design signals are used?

Analyze: typography, spacing, animation, colors, motion, onboarding, language style, illustrations, interaction patterns.

### What level of user sophistication is assumed?

Does the product target: beginners, mainstream users, professionals, technical power users, elite niche communities?

---

## 7. Language and tone analysis

### What vocabulary is repeatedly used?

Look for: recurring words, emotional triggers, identity language, aspirational language, technical language.

### Is the tone

friendly, elite, intellectual, minimalist, corporate, rebellious, futuristic, calming, playful, technical?

### What kind of social status does the tone imply?

Examples: premium, hacker culture, startup culture, intellectual culture, creative class, luxury tech, mass-market friendliness.

---

## 8. Cultural positioning

### What subculture or tribe does the product appeal to?

Examples: startup founders, developers, designers, productivity enthusiasts, self-improvement communities, AI enthusiasts, creatives, intellectual minimalists.

### What internet-era aesthetics or values does it align with?

Examples: minimalism, cyberpunk, AI-native culture, calm tech, creator economy, anti-corporate aesthetics, premium simplicity.

---

## 9. Competitive perception analysis

### How is this product trying to differ from competitors emotionally?

Not feature differences. Emotional differences.

Examples: calmer, smarter, more modern, more technical, more human, more elite, more creative.

### What emotional territory is the company trying to own?

Examples: clarity, creativity, focus, intelligence, speed, simplicity, power, mindfulness.

---

## 10. Contradictions and weaknesses

Identify inconsistencies.

Ask:

- Does the branding conflict with the UX?
- Does the messaging feel fake?
- Does the tone feel forced?
- Is the product over-positioned?
- Is there cognitive dissonance?
- Does the identity promise exceed reality?

---

## 11. Final synthesis

Produce a high-level synthesis. Summarize:

### Core positioning

In 1–2 sentences.

### Emotional core

What emotion is central?

### Identity layer

What identity is being sold?

### Narrative layer

What worldview or story exists?

### Framing strategy

How perception is being shaped.

### Psychological mechanisms

What human motivations are activated?

### Why the product resonates

Why people emotionally connect with it.

### Key branding lessons

What can product founders learn from this example?
