# Bernays (*Propaganda*) for marketing practice

## Description
A concise take on Edward Bernays’ *Propaganda* (1928) focused on **marketing**: how to shape messaging, creative, and positioning so they support **brand choice, product choice, and action** (click, lead, purchase). The ideas come from classic mass‑persuasion logic; for a marketer agent they are a **tool to dissect and sharpen messages**, not a standalone topic about politics or propaganda in general.

## What to use in practice

### Core idea for communications
In broad‑reach ads and content, people often respond to a **recognisable image and emotional framing** faster than to a dry list of facts. Facts matter for compliance and trust, but **interest and memorability** are usually driven by image, meaning, and social context.

### Five levers to audit and improve a message
Use as a **checklist** across creative, landing pages, banners, posts, scripts:

1. **Authority** — experts, studies, standards, certifications, “how the industry recommends it.”
2. **Social proof** — reviews, user numbers, ratings, “chosen by most,” case studies.
3. **Problem / “enemy” in copy** — not a person but a **pain**: fatigue, loss, risk, inefficiency, “the old way.” Gives a simple hook for the offer.
4. **Repetition** — consistent visual and verbal system, multiple touchpoints, taglines; recognition supports brand trust.
5. **Simplicity** — one main idea and one clear next step (CTA), without abstract overload.

Apply all of these **within truthful product claims** and the project’s ethical guardrails.

### Product–image–meaning framework
A reusable structure for positioning and creative:

| Layer | Question for the marketer agent |
|--------|--------------------------------|
| **Product / service** | What are we selling and which customer job does it solve? |
| **Image** | What visual and narrative image does the message carry (hero, lifestyle, status, care)? |
| **Meaning** | Which value are we attaching: success, freedom of choice, safety, belonging, time saved, etc.? |

If the link **product ↔ image ↔ meaning** is weak or forced, the message loses to competitors and converts worse.

### Short market‑oriented notes from Bernays-style thinking
- Grow a weaker category position by attaching a **new social meaning** (not only a new product fact—a new **role** for the product in the audience’s life).
- Build trust with **authority-led claims** and citable sources (surveys, experts)—always **verifiable**, no invented data.
- Premium and strong brands often lean on **status and success imagery**, not on specs alone.

## How the marketer agent should apply this (steps)

1. **Brief** — lock product, ICP/persona, desired action.
2. **Triad** — fill **product–image–meaning** for one core message.
3. **Five-lever checklist** — note what exists; what’s missing for clarity and trust.
4. **Simplify** — one main thought, one CTA per screen/section.
5. **Consistency** — repeat campaign elements (visuals, benefit phrasing).

## Examples (marketing only)

**Deconstruct someone else’s campaign.** Write out the triad and which levers are used; suggest gaps for the goal (awareness / trust / urgency to act).

**Weak landing page.** Check for a clear pain, authority or social proof, and a simple next step; strengthen the weak layer without changing true product facts.
